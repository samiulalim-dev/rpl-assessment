History of The Liberation war

The Bangladesh Liberation War of 1971 was a defining moment in the nation’s history. It was a nine-month-long struggle that resulted in the independence of Bangladesh from Pakistan. Millions of people sacrificed their lives for freedom, justice, and dignity. The war saw immense courage from freedom fighters and civilians alike who resisted oppression and fought for a sovereign nation where equality and humanity could prevail. The victory on December 16, 1971, marked not just the birth of a new country but also the triumph of resilience, unity, and the indomitable spirit of the Bangladeshi people.

Proclamation of Independence

In the first general election in Pakistan, in December 1970, the Bangladesh Awami League (BAL) won nearly every seat representing East Pakistan. That gave them an absolute majority in the National Assembly. President Yahya Khan, however, kept them from taking power by postponing the convening of the assembly indefinitely. Tensions mounted; by early March 1971, there was broad support in East Pakistan for independence...

Official Correspondence: Mujibnagar

"Official Correspondence: Mujibnagar" likely refers to a compiled volume or set of official documents, letters, and communications issued by the Mujibnagar Government (the Provisional Government of Bangladesh in exile) during the 1971 Liberation War. This collection serves as a historical record of the administrative and diplomatic efforts of the nascent Bangladeshi government during the war.

Surrender Documents

The primary surrender document for the Liberation War is the Instrument of Surrender, signed on December 16, 1971, at the Ramna Race Course in Dhaka. It formalized the surrender of the Pakistan Eastern Command to the joint forces of Bangladesh and India. Signed by Lieutenant General A. A. K. Niazi of Pakistan and Lieutenant General Jagjit Singh Aurora of the Allied Forces, the document legally ended the war and enabled the birth of Bangladesh.
